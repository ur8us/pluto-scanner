The central idea

Preserve continuity while everything changes.

The series is less about adding isolated features than removing interruptions. The user should be able to move from gigahertz to hertz, from hopping to fixed reception, and from one processing resolution to another without losing context. Even a disconnected receiver should be a pause, not a dead end.

Principle 01

AI-first development

Choose projects that become feasible because AI changes the economics of making them.

For a specialist with deep C and DSP knowledge but limited web and GitHub experience, AI bridges the missing layers. It also moves useful work away from the desk and gives repetitive browser testing to agents that can run for hours. Human judgment still owns the interface and the tests that require real perception.

Principle 02

A web interface that minimizes traffic

Put the DSP near the radio; send the browser only the pixels’ worth of data it asked for.

ADALM-Pluto feeds samples to a compact C backend. The server computes FFT results, then streams spectrum and waterfall rows over Server-Sent Events. The browser controls both the number of displayed bins and update cadence, so portability does not require wasteful transport.

Principle 03

One spectrum, one million times zoom

Do not force a choice between panoramic awareness and fine spectral inspection.

One continuous view spans from almost the Pluto’s entire 70 MHz–6 GHz tuning range down to hertz-per-pixel detail. The demonstrations move through QO-100 transponders, individual beacons, CW and FT signals, broadcast FM, DAB, and cellular bands. At extreme zoom, frequency resolution improves while time resolution necessarily slows.

Principle 04

Seamless scan and fixed reception

The operator changes scale; the backend chooses the appropriate acquisition mode.

A wide view is assembled by hopping across overlapping frequency steps. As the view narrows, the hop count falls until a single-frequency stream can cover the span continuously. Zoom back out and hopping resumes. The waterfall keeps the transition visually quiet, so internal mode switching never becomes a separate user workflow.

Principle 05

Specify a useful speed range

Let users state the behavior they want, then adapt the processing plan to meet it.

Traditional controls expose FFT-dependent presets whose real cadence is hard to predict. Pluto Scanner instead asks for minimum and maximum waterfall lines per second. The lower bound prevents very fine resolution from feeling frozen; the upper bound protects CPU and network capacity. Some hop-mode limits are physical, so the backend does its best within the range.

Principle 06

Do not erase the past

A view change should reinterpret recorded data, not throw it away.

Waterfall history survives zooming, panning, frequency changes, and canvas resizing. Rows that still overlap the new view remain, stretched or compressed into the new coordinate system. This is more than retaining a bitmap: the history has to stay aligned with frequency while the visible span changes around it.

Principle 07

Change resolution without the wait

Use compatible history to bridge the delay while a high-resolution live pipeline fills.

At maximum zoom, one fresh FFT row can require many seconds of samples. Instead of showing a blank pause, the scanner recalculates stored compatible samples through the new decimation, windowing, and FFT plan. Cached preview rows appear promptly and are released at the intended cadence until live acquisition catches up.

Preview, then liveFast response without pretending processing is free

Principle 08

Plot signals where they really are

Model deterministic tuner quantization instead of accepting it as mysterious offset.

Floating-point arithmetic is only one source of frequency error. PLL and DDS-style tuning words also round requested values to what hardware can represent. By modelling Pluto’s frequency synthesis and carrying fractional-hertz coordinates through the pipeline, the scanner compensates the expected offset without changing the requested LO.

Principle 09

Recover automatically

Treat temporary hardware and backend loss as a state the system can survive.

Unplug the Pluto, reconnect it, restart the backend, or even launch without a receiver present: the application keeps polling and resumes when the device becomes available. The frontend remains coherent through backend restarts. Recovery belongs in the normal operating model, not behind a restart button.

What the series proposes

The complexity can be real without feeling complicated.

Pluto Scanner’s strongest principle is the combination of all nine: keep the operator’s mental model stable while software continuously changes acquisition mode, resolution, rate, coordinates, cached data, and device state underneath.
